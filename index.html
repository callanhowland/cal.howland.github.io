I am a postdoctoral fellow at the Rutgers Center for Cognitive Science.

I defended my dissertation in Philosophy at Rutgers University in Summer 2024 under Elisabeth Camp.

Broadly, I work on the role of perspective sensitivity in lexical processing by thinking about the ways that socially rich linguistic items, like deadnames, chosen names, pronouns, slurs challenge traditional theory of communication.

I think about this work in three parts:

(i) By theorizing an intersubjective perspective that speakers employ in the use of perspective sensitive items (PSIs) which is takes into account the relative prominence and strength of interlocutors' positions and how relatively incontrovertable a piece of information is in context.

(ii) Exploring the ways in which normative harm arises loaded language (e.g. slurs, deadnames, thick terms) in conversation from either breakdowns in or intentional violations of this intersubjective perspective

(iii) And then stepping back and looking more broadly at the social structures that govern these conversational dynamics, and their ethical implications in social access such as technology for the disabled (e.g. assistive technology for disabled people) and structural barriers to transgender people (e.g. name changes and pronoun mistakes)
